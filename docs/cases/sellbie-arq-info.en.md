## Problem

### Navigation that grew with no criterion

The platform's project backlog was prioritized by business value and team capacity, which makes total sense for a growing product. The problem was that this delivery pace wasn't paired with a process for thinking about navigation: each new module found a place in the header as it arrived, and groupings formed by delivery proximity, not by usage logic.

Information Architecture is a specific discipline, and without that lens the structure grows organically until it starts creating real friction for whoever uses it. In practice, that translated into a team with no clear criterion for deciding where to fit each new feature, and users who had to know the product's history to find what they needed.

Over time, the groupings became less and less intuitive. The team noticed the header no longer reflected how the product actually worked, and the request came in: we needed to reorganize. I joined to facilitate this process.

---

## Discovery

### An inventory that revealed the diagnosis

The starting point was mapping what existed: every screen and feature on the platform surveyed in a content inventory in FigJam. Before proposing any new structure, we needed clarity on what needed to be organized.

The inventory confirmed what was already noticeable in use: the header's groupings had formed by delivery order, with no navigation criterion behind them. There was nothing wrong with the decisions that had been made. There simply wasn't an established practice of thinking about navigation as part of the process. It was something that still needed to be built.

This diagnosis guided the next steps: before reorganizing, it made sense to build a shared language around information architecture — understanding which principles guide good navigation decisions and how to apply them in practice.

## Methodological foundation

### Abby Covert's heuristics as a shared foundation

Before moving to execution, it made sense to build a common foundation. Information Architecture has its own principles and vocabulary. Without that repertoire, grouping decisions end up more intuitive than intentional. I wanted everyone to know what they were doing and why before organizing a single card.

To do this, I structured and facilitated a presentation of Abby Covert's heuristics as a step before card sorting. These are 10 principles for evaluating and guiding navigation decisions in practice:

**Findable · Accessible · Clear · Communicative · Useful · Credible · Controllable · Valuable · Learnable · Delightful**

With this repertoire in hand, participants arrived at card sorting understanding what qualities a good navigation structure needs. This made grouping choices more conscious and the discussion of results richer.

## Card Sorting

### Consensus and divergence as decision material

With the inventory mapped and participants equipped, we ran card sorting sessions with 6 people selected for their deep knowledge of the platform, representing product, data, and design. Each participant received cards with the features and organized them into groups that made sense, naming each group freely.

The format chosen was open card sorting, with no pre-defined categories, to reveal the real mental models without contamination from the existing structure.

Analysis of the sessions revealed where there was natural consensus, with features everyone grouped the same way, and where there was real ambiguity, with items migrating between groups depending on who was organizing them. The points of divergence were the most valuable: they weren't errors, they were signals that the label or scope of that feature needed more clarity before any navigation decision. These cases became explicit discussions instead of tacit decisions made with no criterion.

## Structure proposal

### Reorganization based on the real mental model

With the card sorting data analyzed, I put together the proposal for reorganizing the platform's header and navigation architecture. Decisions were made based on three criteria: where there was clear consensus in the groupings, how frequently each feature was used, and how to resolve divergent cases, positioned according to the most recurring journey, not delivery logic.

**New header grouping**
Sections reorganized based on the consensus clusters from card sorting, with labels revised to reflect the mental model identified. Features that generated divergence were positioned based on the main task that motivated access, creating consistency without needing perfect consensus.

**Hierarchy by frequency of use**
Recurring-use items in the main header; settings and support moved to a shortcuts area, reducing the cognitive weight of the main navigation without removing access to anything.

**Updated content inventory**
Every feature mapped with its proposed position, hierarchy level, and group membership. It became a reference document for the team: where to fit new modules, how to name them, what groups with what, preventing the problem from repeating with every new delivery.

## Outcome

### Navigation implemented and a shared criterion across the team

**Navigation reorganized and implemented**
The proposal left FigJam and went to the platform. The header now reflects how the product actually works, organized by mental model instead of delivery order.

**Team with a shared criterion**
For the first time, there was an explicit, documented rationale for where each feature should sit, available for anyone on the team to consult when making navigation decisions.

**Foundation to scale**
The content inventory and grouping criteria now work as a living reference for future decisions, breaking the cycle of navigation growing with no structure.
