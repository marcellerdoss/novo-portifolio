## Why episodic research fails

Most teams do research like this: a problem surfaces, the team requests a study, the study takes three weeks, the findings arrive too late to influence the decision that was already made.

This cycle repeats because research is treated as a project, not a practice.

## What is continuous research?

Continuous research is the practice of having regular conversations with users — weekly or biweekly — in a structured and systematic way, regardless of whether there's an urgent question or not.

The result? When an urgent question does arise, you already have context, established relationships with users, and often answers you didn't even know you needed.

## How to implement it in 4 steps

### 1. Build a participant panel

The biggest bottleneck in ad hoc research is recruiting participants. Build in advance a list of users willing to participate in regular sessions.

Useful tools: Dovetail, Notion, or even a well-organized spreadsheet.

### 2. Define the cadence and format

I recommend 30-minute sessions, every two weeks, with two to three users per week. Enough to collect insights without overwhelming the team.

The format can vary: open-ended interviews, prototype walkthroughs, quick usability tests.

### 3. Distribute the responsibility

Continuous research shouldn't live only with the designer or researcher. PMs and engineers who participate in sessions make better decisions.

### 4. Create an insights repository

Every session generates insights that need to be captured and accessible. Use tags to categorize by theme, feature, and persona.

## What happens after 3 months

You start seeing patterns that would be invisible in one-off research. Recurring problems become obvious. Opportunities emerge naturally.

And the best part: the team stops debating opinions and starts debating evidence.
