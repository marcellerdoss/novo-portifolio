## Onboarding as a contract

Onboarding is not a tutorial. It's the moment when the product makes a promise to the user and delivers — or fails to deliver — its value proposition for the first time.

A poor onboarding doesn't just frustrate the user: it breaks the contract before the relationship even begins.

## Principle 1: Delay sign-up

The most common mistake I see repeatedly: requiring sign-up before showing any value. The user doesn't yet know whether creating an account is worth it.

Show the product first. Let the user experience enough to want to stay. Then ask for the sign-up.

## Principle 2: Reduce Time to Value (TTV)

TTV — Time to Value — is the time between the user arriving at the product and having their first "this works" moment.

Every extra step before that moment is an opportunity to abandon. Map the minimum flow needed to get there.

## Principle 3: Personalize without asking too much

Personalization improves the experience, but long forms push users away. The rule I follow: never ask for more than three pieces of information at once, and only ask for what you're going to use immediately.

## Principle 4: Educate through action

Tooltips and welcome modals are rarely read. People learn by doing.

Instead of explaining what each button does, guide the user to click the right button at the right moment, with just enough context to understand why.

## Principle 5: Define success clearly

How do you know your onboarding is working? Pick an activation metric — the behavior that indicates the user has truly understood the product — and optimize for it.

In the mental health app I redesigned, the metric was "completed the first exercise". Everything in the onboarding pointed toward that moment.

## The result

Applying these five principles in the onboarding redesign, the activation rate went from 32% to 74% in two months. Churn in the first month dropped 40%.

The product didn't change. The way it was presented did.
